En 1978, Polonia ya estaba al borde del levantamiento social.

In 1978, Poland was already on the verge of social survey.

Desde las revueltas obreras de 1970 y una vez más con las de 1976, ambas provocadas por los drásticos incrementos de los precios de los alimentos, el primer secretario Edward Gierek se había esforzado por evitar el descontento interno: sobre todo, como hemos comprobado, endeudándose enormemente con créditos extranjeros y utilizándolos para proporcionar a los polacos alimentos subvencionados y otros artículos de consumo.

From the 1970 workers' revolts and once again with those of 1976, both caused by the drastic increases in food prices, the first secretary Edward Gierek had strived to avoid internal discontent: above all, as we have checked, borrowing greatly with foreign credits and using them to provide polish subsidized foods and other consumer items.

Thanks to the emergence of the Kor of Jacek Kuro, the intellectual opposition and the workers' leaders now cooperated much more than before.

As a reaction to the cautious appearance of "free" unions (that is, illegal) in various industrial and coastal cities, initiated in Katowice and GDAńsk, Kor leaders drafted a letter of workers' rights in December 1979: among their demands the right to constitute autonomous unions, outside the party, and the right to strike, and the right to strike were included.

As expected, the authorities responded to stop intellectual activists and fire the workers who violated legality, including Lech Wałęsa, an electrician then unknown, and other fourteen employees of Elektromontaż in Gdańsk.

What unleashed mass support was an initiative of the Communist Party, which, for the third time in a decade, tried to resolve its economic difficulties announcing, on July 1, 1980, an immediate increase in the price of the meat.

A day after the announcement, Kor declared "Information Agency on the strike."

During the next three weeks the protest strikes were extended from the Ursus tractor factory (1976 Revolt Scenaries) to the main industrial towns of the country, reaching Gdańsk and Lenin shipyards on August 2.

There, the naval workers occupied the facilities and constituted an illegal union, Solidarność (solidarity), directed by Wałęsa, which on August 14, 1980 climbed into the wall that surrounded the shipyards and took the direction of a national strike movement.

The authorities, once defeated their instinctive response - dwells the leaders and isolate the strikers - chose to gain time and divide their opponents.

In an unusual initiative, members of the Politburó were sent to Gdańsk to negotiate with "reasonable" workers' leaders, although at the same time Kuro, Adam Michnik and other Kor leaders were temporarily arrested to interrogate them.

But other intellectuals, such as the historian Bronisław Geremek or the Catholic lawyer Tadeusz Mazowiecki, arrived in Gdańsk to help negotiate the strikers, and they insisted that they would only accept the representation of spokesmen chosen by themselves: especially Wałęsa, an increasingly prominent figure.

On September 1, the Police released the rest of the detainees and two weeks later the Polish State Council officially accepted the main demand of the strikers: the right to constitute and register free unions.

At eight weeks, the informal network of strikes and unions established for the occasion, which now proliferated throughout Poland, had merged into a single organization whose existence the authorities could no longer pretend that they denied: on November 10, 1980 Solidarity became the first officially registered independent union in a communist country, and it was calculated that it had ten million affiliates.

In his National Constitutive Congress of the month of September, Wałęsa was elected president.

Between November 1980 and December 1981 Poland lived in a stimulant and uncomfortable Limbo.

Jacek Kuroń, with the memory of 1956 and 1968 very present, insisted on maintaining support for the "socialist system" and reiterated acceptance by solidarity of the "governing role of the party": nobody wanted to give the Warsaw or Moscow authorities an excuse to send the tanks.
