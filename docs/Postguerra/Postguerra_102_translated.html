Starting from basic social and economic protection programs, the welfare states advanced towards rights systems, benefits, social justice and income redistribution - and managed this essential transformation without virtually any political cost.

The 1930 generation was enough to reach economic security, and, therefore, he turned his back on the political mobilization and his associated risks; His children, the much more numerous generation of 1960, had only known peace, political stability and the welfare state.

The increase in the influence of the State on the employment and well -being of its citizens was accompanied by a constant reduction of their authority over their morals and opinions.

At that time this was not paradoxical.

The liberal and social democratic defenders of the European welfare state did not see in principle reason why the Government should not pay attention to the economic or medical welfare of the population and guarantee the well -being of citizens of the cradle to the burial, while maintaining at the same time completely outside personal issues such as religion and sex, or tastes and artistic criteria.

The Christian -democrats of Germany or Italy, for whom the State still had a legitimate interest in the education and customs of their citizens, could not repair so easily in this distinction.

Until the early 1960s, public authorities from all over Western Europe (with the partial exception of Scandinavia) had exercised firm and repressive control over the matters and private opinions of citizenship.

In Great Britain and France some of the strictest limitations in the field of behavior and expression of opinions had been introduced during the first or Second World War, and never since then had been revoked.

Everywhere - the Western Germany and some of the countries they had occupied - the regulations of the post -war were a legacy of the fascist laws that democratic legislators had preferred to keep in force.

A relatively scarce number of the most repressive "moral" powers that were still in force in 1960 dated dates prior to the nineteenth century (perhaps the most evidently anachronistic was that of the Lord Chambelán office of Great Britain, responsible for the previous censorship of the theater, where the positions of examiner and assistant of the works examiner had already been created in 1738).

La excepción más destacada a esta regla, por supuesto, la constituía la Iglesia Católica.

The most prominent exception to this rule, of course, was the Catholic Church.

Even after the first Vatican council of 1870, held under the influence and auspices of the declared reactionary Pope Pius IX, the Catholic Church had adopted an omnimoda and decisively dogmatic vision of its responsibilities as moral guardian of its flock.

Precisely because the modern State continued to maintain it systematically separated from the scope of political power, the Vatican was inflexible in the demands that his followers required by other means.

In fact, the length and-in retrospect-controversial papacy of Eugenio Pacelli, Pope Pius XII (1939-1958), not only maintained the same spiritual principles, but also introduced the official church in politics again.

Abiertamente situado en el bando de la reacción política, desde los estrechos lazos del Vaticano con Mussolini y su ambivalente respuesta al nazismo hasta su entusiasmo por los dictadores católicos de España y Portugal, el papado de Pacelli también adoptó una línea inflexible en la política doméstica de las democracias.

Openly located on the side of the political reaction, from the narrow ties of the Vatican with Mussolini and his ambivalent response to Nazism until his enthusiasm for the Catholic dictators of Spain and Portugal, Pacelli's papacy also adopted an inflexible line in the domestic policy of democracies.

A los católicos de Italia, especialmente, no les quedaba lugar a dudas sobre la como mínimo incorrección espiritual de votar en contra de los cristianodemócratas; pero incluso en las relativamente liberales Bélgica u Holanda la jerarquía católica local tenía estrictas instrucciones de captar el voto católico para los partidos católicos y solo para ellos.

Tras la muerte de Pacelli, su sucesor, el papa Juan XXIII, convocó un nuevo Concilio Vaticano para abordar estas dificultades y actualizar las actitudes y las prácticas de la Iglesia.

After Pacelli's death, his successor, Pope John XXIII, convened a new Vatican Council to address these difficulties and update the attitudes and practices of the Church.

The Vatican II, as it was called, was convened on October 11, 1962.
