Religion, especially the Catholic religion, enjoyed a brief restoration of its authority.

In Spain, the Catholic hierarchy had both the media and the political support necessary to relaunch its counter -reform: through a concordat signed in 1953, Franco not only left the Church exempt from tax obligations and any other type of state interference, but also granted him the right to exercise his censorship about any writing or speech that was not liked.

A cambio, la jerarquía eclesiástica mantenía y fomentaba la fusión conservadora de la religión con la identidad nacional.

In return, the ecclesiastical hierarchy maintained and encouraged the conservative fusion of religion with national identity.

De hecho, la Iglesia llegó a estar tan absolutamente integrada en los anales de la identidad y el deber nacional que el libro de texto básico de primaria, Yo soy español (editado por primera vez en 1943), enseñaba la historia como una sucesión de acontecimientos única y concatenada que arrancaba con el jardín del Edén y terminaba con el Generalísimo[3].

In fact, the Church became so absolutely integrated in the annals of the identity and the national duty that the basic primary textbook, I am Spanish (first edited in 1943), taught history as a succession of unique and concatenated events that started with the garden of Eden and ended with the Generalissimo [3].

In the thousands of commemorative enclaves dedicated to the victims of anticlerical republicanism, the Spanish Church organized countless ceremonies and acts in their memory.

Una acertada mezcla de religión, autoridad cívica y conmemoración victoriosa reforzaba el monopolio de la jerarquía eclesiástica sobre el espíritu y la memoria de la gente.

A successful mixture of religion, civic authority and victorious commemoration reinforced the monopoly of the ecclesiastical hierarchy about people's spirit and memory.

As in Flanders, the Church in Italy reaped quite successful, especially among monarchists, women and the elderly, a clear majority of the total population.

Of the traditional religions of Europe, only Catholics increased the number of their voters during the 1940s and 1950s.
