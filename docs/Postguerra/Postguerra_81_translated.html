On October 29, two weeks after the United Nations Security Council failed when finding a solution to the Suez crisis (because of the Soviet veto), and only one week after the Sèvres meeting, the Israeli forces entered the Sinai.

The next day, British and French aircraft attacked the Egyptian airfields.

Great Britain, whose reservations of sterling pounds and dollars had declined to 279 million dollars during the crisis, obtained the promise of receiving US financial help (in the form of a credit line of 500 million dollars from the export and import bank of the United States government); On December 10, the IMF announced that it would approve a loan of 561.47 million dollars in favor of Great Britain and a purchase commitment operation of 738 million dollars more.

Israel, after ensuring the United States commitment to its transit right to the Gulf of Aqaba and the Strait of Tyran, withdrew his troops from Gaza the first week of March 1957.

The eviction of the Suez channel began a week later, once the AngloFrancesa withdrawal was over, and the channel was reopened on April 10, 1957, remaining in the hands of Egypt.

All the countries involved learned their own lesson from the Suez Debacle.

Suez's crisis coincided almost exactly with the Soviet occupation of Hungary.

And what is worse, had provided Moscow for unprecedented propaganda asset.
