After all, Hungary was the communist state in which the first Hilton hotel was opened behind the steel curtain in December 1976; where Billy Graham (the American evangelical preacher) made not one but three tours of public appearances during the eighties, and the one that was visited (and implicitly favored) by two US secretaries of state and by the vice president of the United States George Bush in the same decade.

The Catalizer of the change in Hungary was the frustration felt by the young and reformist communists - clearly excited about the transformations that Gorbachev was introducing in the PCUS - before the inflexibility of the elderly leaders of their own party.

Las consecuencias estrictamente prácticas de esta especie de golpe de Estado interno se limitaron a un programa de austeridad económica destinado a robustecer las «fuerzas de mercado», pero tuvo una gran fuerza simbólica.

The strictly practical consequences of this kind of internal coup d'etat were limited to an economic austerity program aimed at robusting "market forces", but had a great symbolic force.

János Kádár había gobernado Hungría desde la revolución de 1956, en cuya represión había tenido el papel principal.

János Kádár had ruled Hungary since the 1956 revolution, in whose repression he had had the main role.

A pesar de tener una imagen exterior bastante positiva, para los húngaros encarnaba la mentira oficial en la que se sustentaba el «comunismo del gulash», según la cual el movimiento reformista húngaro no había sido más que una contrarrevolución.

Despite having a rather positive exterior image, for the Hungarians embodied the official lie in which the "communism of the Gulash" was supported, according to which the Hungarian reformist movement had only been a counterrevolution.

Kádár was also the living symbol of the conspiracy of silence that surrounded Imre Nagy since his kidnapping, secret judgment and, above all, execution and burial clandestine three decades before [19].

Therefore, the fact of separating Kádár from power seemed to indicate that something fundamental had changed in the public life of the country, an impression that was confirmed when their successors not only allowed a group composed in part by young communist dissidents to constitute Fidesz (young Democrats), but also officially authorized, in November 1988, the appearance of independent political parties.

Even greater importance was the fact that Hungary Communist leaders - tacitly recognizing that their party could not rely on maintaining control of the country unless they recognized their past - announced their intention to exhume the problematic remains of Imre Nagy to give them a dignified burial.

On June 16, 1989 - in the thirty -first anniversary of his death - the remains of Imre Nagy and four of his colleagues were ceremoniously buried as national heroes.

Among the speakers who talked to the grave was Viktor Orbán, a young leader of the young Democrats, who could not help pointing out that some of the communists present in Nagy's second burial were the same as a few years ago had falsified the revolution that at that time they were praising.

Una de las peculiaridades del fin del comunismo en Hungría es que lo dirigieran los propios comunistas: hasta junio no se convocaron mesas redondas de conversaciones, que imitaban conscientemente el ejemplo polaco.

One of the peculiarities of the end of communism in Hungary is to be directed by the communists themselves: until June no round tables of conversations were convened, which consciously imitated the Polish example.

Esto produjo cierto escepticismo entre los anticomunistas húngaros, para los que la resurrección de Nagy, al igual que anteriormente su ejecución, era un problema interno del partido que apenas tenía importancia para las muchas víctimas del comunismo.

For its part, Parliament, still composed in its vast majority by communist deputies chosen during the former Single Party Regime, approved on October 23 that the country was simply called the Republic of Hungary.

La «revolución» húngara de 1989 presentó dos rasgos característicos.

The Hungarian "revolution" of 1989 presented two characteristic features.

El primero, como hemos visto, es que fue el único tránsito desde un régimen comunista a un auténtico sistema multipartidista realizado completamente desde dentro.

El segundo es que, mientras que en Polonia, al igual que posteriormente en Checoslovaquia y en los demás países del Este, los acontecimientos de 1989 fueron en gran medida autorreferenciales, la transición húngara tuvo un papel clave en el desenlace de otro régimen comunista, el de Alemania Oriental.

The second is that, while in Poland, as later in Czechoslovakia and in the other East countries, the 1989 events were largely self -referential, the Hungarian transition had a key role in the outcome of another communist regime, that of Eastern Germany.
