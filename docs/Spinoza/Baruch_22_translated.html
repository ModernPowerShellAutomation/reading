We first agree on an essential point: of course Spinoza does not believe in a personal and creative God, namely that of monotheistic religions.

But, where our points of view diverge is that you think that Spinoza uses (very frequently) the word God as a precaution and that, since the definition he gives is very far from monotheistic conception, the word is emptied of its substance and is only used for prudence.

J’ai aussi maintes fois rappelé ici la prudence de Spinoza, qui a publié son Traité théologico-politique de manière anonyme, qui a renoncé à publier l’ Éthique de son vivant, et je n’ai pas manqué non plus de rappeler qu’il avait choisi pour devise le mot latin Caute, « Méfie-toi2 ».

I have also repeatedly recalled here the prudence of Spinoza, who published his theological-political treaty in an anonymous way, which gave up publishing the ethics during his lifetime, and I did not fail to recall that he had chosen the word Latin Caute, "Misfy2".

But I remain convinced, however, that he did not write anything that he really thought, that it was on God as on any other subject.

Et si la question de Dieu était étrangère à sa doctrine, mais un simple artifice pour se concilier les bonnes grâces de ses amis chrétiens et éviter les foudres des autorités religieuses et publiques, que n’avait-il besoin d’y consacrer le premier et le dernier livre de son ouvrage majeur ?

And if God's question was foreign to his doctrine, but a simple artifice to reconcile the good graces of his Christian friends and avoid the wrath of religious and public authorities, what did he need to devote the first and last book of his major work?

Il aurait suffi d’y faire allusion brièvement et de consacrer son livre à sa vision anthropologique et éthique radicalement nouvelle, fondée sur le conatus, le désir et la joie.

It would have been enough to allude briefly and to consecrate his book to his radically new anthropological and ethical vision, based on conatus, desire and joy.

Or il m’apparaît évident que Spinoza a l’ambition de fonder son éthique sur une métaphysique.

However, it seems obvious to me that Spinoza has the ambition to base his ethics on a metaphysics.

Il part de Dieu, cet « être absolument infini « ( Éthique, I, définition) et il affirme que « toutes choses sont en Dieu et dépendent de lui » ( Éthique, I, appendice), avant de descendre dans les profondeurs de la psyché humaine pour revenir à Dieu à travers la libération de la servitude et l’accès à la Béatitude par « l’amour intellectuel de Dieu qui est éternel » ( Éthique, V, 33), dont il parle, de toute évidence, comme une expérience vécue.

He starts from God, this "absolutely infinite being" (ethical, I, definition) and he affirms that "all things are in God and depend on him" (ethics, i, appendix), before descending into the depths of human psyche to return to God through the liberation of servitude and access to bliss by "the intellectual love of God which is eternal" (Ethics, V, 33), which he obviously talks about, as a lived experience.

Il me semble, cher Robert, que vous réduisez la définition de Dieu à celle donnée par les traditions monothéistes occidentales – un être personnel et créateur – ce qui vous conduit logiquement à penser que Spinoza construit son système en parlant d’un Dieu auquel il ne croit pas.

It seems to me, dear Robert, that you reduce the definition of God to that given by Western monotheistic traditions - a personal and creative being - which logically leads you to think that Spinoza builds his system by speaking of a God in which he does not believe.

Or, justement, Spinoza redéfinit de fond en comble le concept de Dieu.

Now, precisely, Spinoza redefines from top to bottom the concept of God.

Et, comme je l’explique dans le chapitre sur « le Dieu de Spinoza », sa vision non dualiste et immanente de Dieu rejoint, de manière étonnante, celle des grandes sagesses de l’Inde ou de la Chine.

And, as I explain in the chapter on "The God of Spinoza", his non -dualist and immanent vision of God joins, in a surprising way, that of the great wisdoms of India or China.

Sans les avoir connues, Spinoza donne une définition du divin qui ressemble fort à celle du brahman hindou ou du Tao chinois.

Without having known them, Spinoza gives a definition of the divine which looks very much like that of the Brahman Hindu or the Chinese Tao.

Belle preuve du caractère universel de la raison humaine !

Great proof of the universal character of human reason!

This divine impersonal and cosmic is not only for Spinoza a reality, but it is even the ultimate reality.

Did he need to call him "God", at the risk of creating confusion with the radically different definition given by his contemporaries in our Judeo-Christian tradition?

This is where I join you: the use of the word God is undoubtedly wanted to alleviate the revolutionary character of his monist metaphysics.

Mais cela, pour moi, ne change rien au fond : Spinoza propose une nouvelle définition de Dieu, qu’il considère comme la plus achevée.

But that, for me, does not change the bottom: Spinoza offers a new definition of God, whom he considers to be the most completed.

Il ne croit pas à la représentation qu’il juge infantile du Dieu auquel ses semblables rendent un culte, mais il pense Dieu comme un être infini, principe de raison et modèle de vie bonne, et cette pensée de « Dieu » l’a mis en joie et a gouverné toute sa vie.

He does not believe in the representation that he deems infantile of the God to whom his fellows make a cult, but he thinks God as an infinite being, a principle of reason and a good model of life, and this thought of "God" put him in joy and ruled his whole life.

Appelons ça de l’« athéisme » si vous voulez, puisque vous entendez rester dans la définition biblique de Dieu, mais je comprends pourquoi Spinoza a toujours récusé ce qualificatif, lui qui avait pour ambition de dépasser la croyance religieuse pour inventer une pensée philosophique de ce que, faute de mieux, on appelle universellement, et très diversement, « Dieu ».

Let us call it "atheism" if you want, since you intend to stay in the biblical definition of God, but I understand why Spinoza has always challenged this qualifier, he who had the ambition to go beyond religious belief to invent a philosophical thought of what, for lack of better, we universally called, and very variously, "God".

Toutefois, afin d’éviter tout malentendu, on pourrait parler de « panthéisme » plutôt que de « théisme », pour qualifier sa conception d’un Dieu identifié à la Nature, ce qui réconcilierait peut-être, en partie, nos deux points de vues ?

However, in order to avoid any misunderstanding, one could speak of "pantheism" rather than "theism", to qualify its conception as a god identified with nature, which would perhaps reconcile our two points of view?
