In 1917, they had used the revolutionary forces put into operation on account of the last war to take power.

In this myth, born from the victory of 1945, other previous historical episodes in which Europe had been "saved" by the selfless sacrifice of Russia: in Kulikovo, when the Moscows defeated the Mongols, or during the war against Napoleon.

The dead were instrumentalized in the conflict to build this messianic myth: they were invoked as proof, not of the inefficiency and insensitive contempt for Soviet human life, but of the sacrifice of Russia, the price he had paid to save humanity.

In February 1946, in a speech that pointed out this return to the planned economy, Stalin said that the war had tested the Soviet system.

His victory left the superiority of the communist multinational state, the Red Army and the planned economy, without which the "value of our soldiers" would not have been enough to defeat the enemy.

During the following forty years, this myth was used to legitimize the Soviet system and justify everything he had achieved since 1917.

Stalin continued to have doubts that the people were going to access the system if a new war with the West occurred.

His main objective during the Cold War was shielding the country in the face of Western influence.

Delirious statements about Russia's greatness began in the Soviet press.

Zóshchenko was the last of the Soviet satirical authors, a literary tradition that dictators cannot bear.

The State Museum of Modern Western Art was closed, and a campaign against "formalism" and other "Western decadent influences" began in Soviet music, which led to inclusion in the blacklist of composers such as Shostakóvich, Jachaturián and Prokófiev, among others.

Accused of "servility" towards the West, they were taken before a "court of honor", a new institution in charge of examining "antipatriotic" acts, where they were forced to answer hostile questions in front of eight hundred spectators.

The following year a campaign began against the "cosmopolitas" (that is, the Jews) throughout the rest of the fields of culture, with expulsions of the party, the union of writers, universities and research institutes.

The idea of the plot originated in 1948, when Lidia Timastuk, a doctor at the Kremlin Hospital, wrote to Stalin two days before the death of Zhdánov, ensuring that the medical staff who attended him had not recognized the seriousness of his situation.

The letter was ignored and filed, but four years later Stalin, by then completely paranoid, used it to accuse the Kremlin doctors of belonging to a "Zionist conspiracy" to kill Zhdánov and the rest of the party's leadership.

Hundreds of doctors and health workers were arrested and tortured until they obtained from them the false confession that belonged to a great international conspiracy that linked the Soviet Jews with Israel and the United States.

The country was returning to the 1937 environment, and this time the role of "enemies of the people" was performed by the Jews.

In December 1952, Stalin said at a meeting of the central committee that "every Jew is a potential spy of the United States."
