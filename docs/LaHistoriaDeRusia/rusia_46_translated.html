Nacido en 1917, Dmitri Streletski era el mayor de los cuatro hijos de una familia campesina que habitaba en la región de Kurgán, en los Urales.

Born in 1917, Dmitri Streletski was the oldest of the four children of a peasant family that lived in the Kurgan region, in Los Urals.

En 1930, los miembros del hogar fueron despojados de sus escasas posesiones y deportados a un campo de trabajo.

In 1930, household members were stripped of their few possessions and deported to a field of work.

El comité regional del Partido había ordenado al sóviet de la aldea que buscara diecisiete familias de kulaks para su deportación, y la suya había sido elegida para completar la cuota.

The Regional Committee of the party had ordered the village's Sóviet to seek seventeen Kulaks families for deportation, and his had been chosen to complete the quota.

Durante años languidecieron en campos y colonias penales, en unas condiciones terribles.

For years they languished in criminal fields and colonies, in terrible conditions.

Dmitri se aplicó con denuedo en el estudio, pero no pudo ir a la universidad ni conservar ningún trabajo adecuado debido a sus orígenes kulaks.

Dmitri applied boldly in the study, but he could not go to university or keep any proper work due to his Kulaks origins.

Aun así, nunca dejó de creer en Stalin, y él mismo era, en sus propias palabras, un «ferviente estalinista».

Even so, he never stopped believing in Stalin, and he himself was, in his own words, a "stalinist fervent."

Décadas más tarde, al hacer repaso de su vida, contó en una serie de entrevistas: «Era más fácil para nosotros [las víctimas de la represión] sobrevivir a nuestro castigo si seguíamos creyendo en Stalin, si seguíamos pensando que a Stalin lo estaban engañando los enemigos del pueblo, y no perdíamos todas las esperanzas que habíamos depositado en él […].

Decades later, when reviewing his life, he said in a series of interviews: «It was easier for us [the victims of repression] survive our punishment if we continued to believe in Stalin, if we continued thinking that Stalin were deceiving him the enemies of the people, and we did not lose all the hopes that we had deposited in him […].

Tal vez fuera una forma de autoengaño, pero, psicológicamente, creer en la justicia de Stalin nos hacía más fácil soportar la vida.

Maybe it was a form of self -deception, but, psychologically, believing in Stalin's justice made us easier to endure life.

The year had begun with generalized strikes.

Los trabajadores protestaban contra los recortes de las raciones de comida que recibían, el alargamiento de las jornadas laborales y la caída de los salarios reales, que se habían reducido a la mitad con respecto a los de 1928.

The workers protested against the cuts of the food rations they received, the lengthening of the working hours and the fall of real wages, which had been reduced by half with respect to those of 1928.

The most dangerous of them had been organized by Martemyan Riutin, an old Bujarin supporter, whose thesis "Stalin and the crisis of the dictatorship of the proletariat", of two hundred pages, circulated between the bases of the party.

Más conocida como «plataforma Riutin», su texto planteaba una crítica furibunda tanto a las políticas como a la personalidad de Stalin, y lo denunciaba como un pensador mediocre, un «intrigante sin escrúpulos» y un «sepulturero de la revolución» que la había enterrado con sus políticas catastróficas y que, al forzar la colectivización, había traicionado los principios voluntaristas de Lenin.

Better known as "Riutin platform," his text raised a furious criticism of both the policies and the personality of Stalin, and denounced him as a mediocre thinker, an "unscrupulous intriguing" and a "grave of the revolution" that had buried it with its catastrophic policies and that, by forcing the collectivization, had betrayed Lenin's voluntary principles.

Otros fueron expulsados del Partido por el simple hecho de tener conocimiento de la existencia del grupo y no haber informado de ello a la OGPU.

Others were expelled from the party for the simple fact of having knowledge of the existence of the group and not having informed the OGPU.

Este episodio dejó a Stalin con la convicción paranoide de que sus «enemigos» estaban en todas partes.

This episode left Stalin with the paranoid conviction that his "enemies" were everywhere.

Dicho convencimiento se afianzó con el suicidio de su mujer, Nadezhda Allilueva, el 8 de noviembre.

This conviction was strengthened with the suicide of his wife, Nadezhda Allilueva, on November 8.

La noche anterior, Stalin había sido desagradable con ella en una cena celebrada en el Kremlin para conmemorar el decimoquinto aniversario de la Revolución de Octubre.

The previous night, Stalin had been unpleasant with her at a dinner held at the Kremlin to commemorate the fifteenth anniversary of the October Revolution.

Sabía que simpatizaba con Bujarin (la mujer de este era la mejor amiga de Nadezhda) y que estaba horrorizada por lo que había descubierto sobre la colectivización a través de los relatos de sus compañeros de estudios de la Academia Industrial.

I knew that she sympathized with Bujarin (the woman of this was Nadezhda's best friend) and that she was horrified by what she had discovered about the collectivization through the stories of her colleagues in the Industrial Academy.

Stalin had proposed a toast for the destruction of the "enemies of the State."

Nadezhda did not lift her glass.

Stalin harassed her and demanded that he explain why he didn't drink.

En abril de 1933 anunció una purga general del Partido.

In April 1933 he announced a general purge of the party.

Fueron expulsados casi uno de cada cinco de los 3,2 millones de miembros que tenía (no solo los «elementos socialmente extraños», como en purgas anteriores, sino aquellos considerados «poco de fiar»).

They were expelled almost one of every five of the 3.2 million members he had (not only the "socially strange elements", as in previous purges, but those considered "little of trust").

Es notable que la dirección del Partido siguiera sintiéndose tan insegura más de quince años después de haber llegado al poder.

It is remarkable that the direction of the party continued to feel so insecure more than fifteen years after having come to power.

Stalin was not able to trust the bases.

Como Iván el Terrible con los boyardos, necesitaba poner constantemente a prueba su lealtad.

As Ivan the terrible with the Boyardos, he needed to constantly put his loyalty to the test.

Deseaba crear su propia élite de trabajadores ascendidos desde las plantas de las fábricas (vidvizhentsi), los cuales le corresponderían con una obediencia total.

He wanted to create his own elite of workers promoted from the plants of the factories (vidvizhentsi), which would correspond to it with total obedience.

Formados como ingenieros y gerentes en escuelas nocturnas, los vidvizhentsi de la década de 1930 se convirtieron en el pilar del régimen estalinista.

Formed as engineers and managers in night schools, the vidvizhentsi of the 1930s became the pillar of the Stalinist regime.

Fueron las purgas de aquellos años las que posibilitaron su ascenso, pues al desaparecer sus superiores pudieron ocupar los puestos de trabajo de estos.

It was the purges of those years that enabled their rise, because when their superiors disappear they were able to occupy their jobs.

En 1952, los vidvizhentsi constituían la mitad de los dirigentes del régimen soviético (57 de los 115 ministros principales del Gobierno), entre ellos Jruschov, Brézhnev, Gromiko y Kosiguin.

In 1952, the Vidvizhentsi constituted half of the leaders of the Soviet regime (57 of the 115 main ministers of the Government), including Jruscov, Brézhnev, Gromiko and Kosiguin.

Para 1934, Stalin creía que había derrotado a sus oponentes.

By 1934, Stalin believed he had defeated his opponents.

Su dictadura personal parecía asegurada.

His personal dictatorship seemed insured.

Entre sus seguidores leales lo conocían como el joziain, «el amo y señor», un término patrimonial que se empleaba para los zares.

Among his loyal followers they knew him as the Joziain, "The Master and Lord," a patrimonial term that was used for the tsars.

El 26 de enero, en la sesión inaugural del XVII Congreso del Partido, afirmaría que los grupos «antileninistas» de oposición habían sido derrotados.

On January 26, in the inaugural session of the XVII Party Congress, he would affirm that the "antileninist" opposition groups had been defeated.

Bujarin y el resto de los que habían cuestionado las políticas de Stalin se retractaron de sus «errores».

Bujarin and the rest of those who had questioned Stalin's policies retracted from their "mistakes."

Pravda called him the "Congress of the victors."

Actually, this congress was the last revolt against Stalin within the rows of the party.

However, it was obvious that the opposition to its policies was growing among the regional secretaries of the party, some of whom wanted to replace the Secretary General for Serguéi Kírov, the popular leader of the party in Leningrad (as he had renamed the city of Petrograd), which had obtained more votes than Stalin.

Since the death of Stalin's wife, Kírov had become someone very close to the leader, practically a family member.

On December 1, he was killed at the Smolny Institute of Leningrad by a discontent member of the party, which was known to be a threat but that the police allowed to enter with a gun and look for Kírov in their offices.

A few hours after the murder, Stalin took control of the investigation and issued an emergency decree that allowed the summary judgments and the execution of alleged "terrorists."

The "people from before" of the city (as the elites of the Tsarist era were called) fell by thousands in the raids.

En la purga del Partido de 1935, se expulsó a un cuarto de millón de sus miembros, la mayoría de ellos investigados por la NKVD (el nuevo nombre de la OGPU a partir de 1934) y acusados de ser «antileninistas».

In the purge of the 1935 party, a quarter of a million of its members was expelled, most of them investigated by the NKVD (the new name of the OGPU from 1934) and accused of being "antileninistas."

La intensa participación de la NKVD en una purga del Partido era una novedad, y estableció la pauta que se seguiría en el Gran Terror.

The intense participation of the NKVD in a purge of the party was a novelty, and established the guideline that would be followed in the great terror.

Stalin was the driving force of this campaign.

In 1936 Yagoda was replaced by Nikolai Yezhov, an unscrupulous henchman who dedicated himself to feeding Stalin's paranoid fears.

The eight were shot a few hours after receiving their conviction.

El ejército había sido la única institución capaz de oponerse a la ambición de Stalin de hacerse con el poder total (razón por la cual el juicio de los generales había sido secreto).

The army had been the only institution capable of opposing Stalin's ambition to take full power (which is why the judgment of the generals had been secret).

En el último y el más grande de estos juicios espectáculo, que tuvo lugar en marzo de 1938, Bujarin, Yagoda y Ríkov, junto con otros trece bolcheviques, fueron condenados a morir fusilados por conspirar con la «organización terrorista Trotski-Zinóviev» para asesinar a los dirigentes soviéticos y espiar a las órdenes de los estados fascistas.

In the last and the greatest of these spectacle trials, which took place in March 1938, Bujarin, Yagoda and Ríkov, along with other thirteen Bolsheviks, were sentenced to die shot for conspiring with the "Trotskyviev terrorist organization" to kill the Soviet leaders and spy on the orders of the fascist states.

Los juicios espectáculo enviaban un mensaje a las filas del Partido: debían denunciar a los presuntos opositores, a cualquiera que tuviera alguna conexión, por antigua o por remota que fuera, con los «enemigos del pueblo».

The spectacle trials sent a message to the ranks of the party: they had to denounce the alleged opponents, to anyone who had any connection, by old or remotely, with the "enemies of the people."

Estos oficiales se protegían unos a otros mientras se mantuviera su círculo de poder, pero la detención de uno de ellos conducía inevitablemente a la del resto de los miembros de la camarilla gobernante, así como de sus secuaces, una vez que la NKVD se ponía a trabajar para desvelar los vínculos entre ellos.

These officers protected each other while maintaining their circle of power, but the arrest of one of them inevitably led to that of the rest of the members of the ruling clique, as well as their henchmen, once the NKVD began to work to reveal the links between them.

Terror extended both among the rows of the party and by the Soviet institutions and society.

Millions of people denounced their colleagues, their neighbors, their friends, sometimes even their relatives.

Some of them were motivated by malice, the desire for revenge, jealousy or the perspective of obtaining some material benefit or promotion.

Others acted by a sense of patriotic duty.

Propaganda were believed about "spies" and "enemies."
